## March CPI Report Analysis and Key Drivers
- **Headline CPI surge driven by energy**: Headline CPI rose by 0.9% month-over-month, the largest increase since January 2022.
  - Primary cause: A 21% increase in gasoline prices following the Iran war, which accounted for approximately 80 basis points of the 90 basis point total increase.
  - Impact on expectations: The University of Michigan one-year inflation expectations survey rose from 3.8% to 4.8%. Long-run expectations increased to 3.4%, a level the Fed is likely to tolerate.
- **Softer Core CPI performance**: Core CPI was weaker than forecasted, rising by 0.20% against an expected 0.26%.
  - Main reason for the forecast miss: Used car prices unexpectedly declined by 0.4%, contrary to a forecasted 1% increase that was based on rising Manheim wholesale data.
- **Evidence of energy price pass-through**: The Fed is closely monitoring the pass-through from higher energy costs to core inflation.
  - Early indicators: Airfare prices rose by 2.7% month-over-month, and delivery services increased by 3.1%, partly attributed to fuel surcharges from entities like USPS and Amazon.
  - Forecast impact: A 30% increase in oil prices is expected to raise year-end core PCE inflation by 30 basis points to 3.1% (revised from a previous 2.8% forecast). This forecast has downside risk if a ceasefire holds and consumer demand weakens.

## Implications for Federal Reserve Policy and PCE Outlook
- **Core PCE translation and year-over-year outlook**: The CPI data provides more clarity on the upcoming Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index.
  - Month-over-month moderation: Core PCE is expected to rise by 0.20% month-over-month, aligning with core CPI and marking a moderation from recent hotter readings.
  - Year-over-year increase: Due to a low base effect from last March (+0.1%), the year-over-year core PCE rate is projected to increase to 3.1% in March from 3.0% in February.
  - Underlying inflation remains high: Underlying inflation is still considered to be above the Fed's 2% target, with tariffs estimated to be contributing 50 to 60 basis points.
- **Fed's expected policy path**: The report is viewed as "better news, but not great news," and is unlikely to alter the Fed's current holding pattern.
  - Rate cuts anticipated: Two rate cuts totaling 50 basis points are still expected for September and October.
  - Rationale for cuts: The Fed is likely to look through inflation driven by tariffs and the war, especially if pass-through effects are limited. A new, more dovish Fed chair is also expected to be in place by September.
- **Technical risk in PCE calculation**: There is a specific uncertainty regarding the legal services component in the PCE calculation.
  - Data inconsistency: The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) has been inconsistent, sometimes using CPI and other times PPI for the legal services component.
  - Potential upside risk: The CPI data showed a sharp 4.1% month-over-month decline in legal services. If the BEA ignores this volatile reading and uses a more muted PPI figure instead, it would pose an upside risk to the core PCE tracking estimate.

## Market Response and Investment Strategy
- **Initial market reaction was muted**: The overall market move in response to the CPI print was small and not sustained.
  - Rates market: A modest bull steepening occurred initially due to the softer core reading, but this move later retraced.
  - FX market: A slight, choppy, knee-jerk depreciation of the dollar was observed post-data, but it was not a discernible or lasting reaction.
- **Conviction view on US rates**: The front end and belly of the US rates curve are considered oversold, with the market having overpriced the Fed's near-term hawkishness.
  - Supporting factors:
    1.  **Term premium**: The recent term premium shock is concentrated at the front end and belly of the curve, a notable difference from the previous year.
    2.  **Positioning**: CTAs are at maximum short levels, while both foreign and domestic investors have pulled back from front-end auctions.
    3.  **Inflation curve**: The inflation curve has flattened significantly, with the front end overreacting to oil prices relative to the limited pass-through seen in forwards.
  - Recommended trades: Long five-year real yield and long five-year, five-year inflation compensation, with the latter also seen as a hedge against the main view.
- **Primary FX driver is geopolitical, not data**: The foreign exchange market is predominantly driven by developments in the Middle East war.
  - Current market theme: A "relief rally" against the dollar is underway following a ceasefire announcement, leading to an unwind of defensive long-dollar positions established in March.
  - Dollar outlook: The dollar is expected to remain choppy in the short term as the market reacts to headlines. The broader view remains for dollar depreciation later in the year, contingent on the Fed initiating an easing cycle.

**Q: What were the key details of the March CPI report and the main drivers behind the numbers?**
A: The key details are as follows:
1.  **Headline CPI:** Rose by 0.9% month-over-month, the largest increase since January 2022. This was almost entirely driven by energy prices.
    *   Gasoline prices increased by 21% following the Iran war, accounting for approximately 80 basis points of the 90 basis point increase in headline CPI.
2.  **Core CPI:** Came in softer than forecast at a 0.20% increase, compared to a forecast of 0.26%.
    *   The main reason for the forecasting miss was used car prices. A 1% increase was expected (based on Manheim wholesale data), but they actually declined by 0.4%. This explains the majority of the miss.
3.  **Energy Price Pass-through:** There were some early signs of higher energy prices passing through to core inflation, which the Fed will be watching closely.
    *   Airfare saw a significant increase of 2.7% month-over-month.
    *   Delivery services rose by 3.1%, influenced by fuel surcharges from providers like USPS and Amazon.
    *   Our forecast now incorporates significant pass-through, raising our year-end core PCE inflation forecast from 2.8% to 3.1% Q4-over-Q4.

**Q: What was the impact of the CPI report on inflation expectations?**
A: The University of Michigan inflation expectations survey showed a material rise, largely due to sensitivity to oil prices.
1.  **One-Year Expectations:** Jumped from 3.8% to 4.8%.
2.  **Long-Run Expectations:** Rose to 3.4%, which was in line with the preliminary reading. The Fed is likely comfortable with this level, as it has not experienced a significant level shift higher and would likely decrease if oil prices normalize.

**Q: What does this CPI report mean for the Federal Reserve's policy and your forecast for future actions?**
A: The report is viewed as better news for the Fed, but not great news, and is unlikely to shift them from their current holding pattern.
1.  **Core PCE Outlook:** We expect core PCE for March to increase by 0.20% month-over-month, directly in line with core CPI. This represents a moderation compared to recent months where core PCE ran hotter than core CPI.
2.  **Year-over-Year Rate:** Despite the sequential improvement, the year-over-year core PCE rate is likely to increase to 3.1% in March from 3.0% in February due to base effects (March of last year saw only a 0.1% increase).
3.  **Underlying Inflation:** Inflation remains above the Fed's 2% target, with tariffs estimated to be contributing 50 to 60 basis points to core PCE.
4.  **Forecast for Fed Action:** We still expect the Fed to eventually cut rates this year.
    *   We have two cuts of 25 basis points each forecasted for September and October (50 basis points total).
    *   This is based on the expectation that the Fed will start to look through inflation caused by tariffs and the war, especially if pass-through is not significant.
    *   A new, more dovish Fed Chair (Kevin Warsh) is expected to be in office by September, which should help steer the committee toward easing.

**Q: How did financial markets, particularly the US rates market, respond to the inflation data, and what is your current strategic view?**
A: The overall market move was small, with an initial modest bull steepening that has since retraced. Our strategic view is as follows:

2.  **Specific Trades:** This is reflected in trades such as being long five-year real yields and long five-year, five-year inflation compensation.
3.  **Rationale:**
    *   The market has overpriced the hawkishness of the Fed's near-term path.
    *   The recent term premium shock has been concentrated at the front end, indicating cheapness.
    *   Positioning data shows CTAs are at maximum short levels on the front end, and there has been a pullback from investors in Treasury auctions for these tenors.
    *   The inflation curve has flattened significantly, and the front-end's reaction to oil prices appears overdone relative to the limited pass-through seen in forwards.
4.  **Key Risk and Hedge:** The main risk to this view is the Fed meaningfully putting rate hikes on the table. However, this risk is mitigated because the Fed focuses on wages (which show little pressure) and long-term inflation expectations (which are low). **Q: You mentioned a specific detail regarding the translation of CPI to PCE. Can you elaborate on this potential risk?**
A: There is a wonkish detail concerning the legal services component that creates uncertainty for the core PCE tracking estimate.
1.  **BEA Methodology:** The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) has been inconsistent in its methodology for this component. In January, it used PPI data instead of CPI data for legal services, effectively ignoring a large pop in the CPI measure. In February, it appeared to revert to using CPI.
2.  **Current Month's Data:** This month's CPI data implies a sharp decline of 4.1% month-over-month in legal services.
3.  **Upside Risk to PCE:** It is uncertain whether the BEA will incorporate this large decline or wait for PPI data, as it faded the earlier increase. If the BEA chooses to ignore the CPI decline and use a more muted PPI figure, it would pose an upside risk to our 0.20% core PCE tracking estimate.
